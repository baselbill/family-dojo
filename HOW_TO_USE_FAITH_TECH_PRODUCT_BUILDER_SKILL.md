# How to Use the Faith-Tech Product Builder Skill

Your faith-tech-product-builder skill is ready to help you build authentic faith-integrated apps. This guide shows how to use it.

## Quick Start (2 minutes)

### Current Method: Copy-Paste the Skill Prompt

Custom skills don't work as slash commands yet. Use this workaround:

**Step 1: Get the skill prompt**
1. Open this file: `.claude/skills/faith-tech-product-builder.md`
2. Copy the entire system prompt section (the "You are a faith-tech product expert..." paragraph)

**Step 2: Paste in a new Claude Code session**
1. Start a new Claude Code session or conversation
2. Paste the system prompt you just copied
3. Press Enter

**Step 3: Describe your faith-tech idea**

The skill will ask clarifying questions and guide you through building your app. Example prompts:

```
We're building a prayer app for families (kids + parents). 
Target: make prayer feel like natural rhythm, not checklist. 
How do we design this so it doesn't become legalistic/shame-based?
```

OR

```
We want to create a Bible reading plan app for ages 11-16 with streak tracking, 
progress milestones, and community features. How do we integrate scripture 
authentically without being preachy? What theological perspective should we take?
```

OR

```
We're designing a values-tracking app for families based on Christian virtues 
(kindness, courage, honesty). We want kids to reflect on living out these values, 
not earn rewards. How should we design this?
```

### What Happens Next

The skill will:
1. **Clarify your vision** (~2-3 min) — What problem are you solving? Who are users? What's your faith perspective?
2. **Assess theologically** (~5 min) — Is this spiritually sound? Any red flags (shame, legalism, proof-texting)?
3. **Design mechanics** (~10 min) — Features that honor faith, not exploit it
4. **Align with values** (~5 min) — Ensure mechanics match your faith tradition
5. **Plan community strategy** (~5 min) — Marketing, advisory board, beta testing
6. **Handle sensitive topics** (~5 min) — Shame, doubt, abuse, LGBTQ+, grief, etc.
7. **Provide roadmap** (~5 min) — MVP, phases, key partnerships, success metrics

**Total time**: 30-45 minutes for a complete faith-tech product plan

## Real Examples

### Example 1: Prayer Habit Tracker

**Your Question:**
```
We're building a prayer app for families (kids + parents, ages 8-14). 
We want prayer to feel like a natural rhythm, not a checklist. 
Our faith tradition emphasizes that prayer is conversation with God, not performance. 
We're worried about creating legalism or shame if kids miss days. 
How do we design this authentically?
```

**Skill Output** (summary):

- **Theological Assessment**: ✅ Good foundation (conversation-based theology avoids transactional framing)
  - Red flag to avoid: Streaks reset = condemnation language
  - Strength to build on: Prayer as relationship, not works

- **Mechanic Design**:
  - Track frequency, but not as performance metric
  - Grace-period: "Missed a few days? That's ok. Come back when you're ready. God's grace is new each morning."
  - No shame: No public failure, no reset penalties
  - Reflection > achievement: Journaling prompts, conversation starters for families
  - Community: Family prayer circle (optional), prayer request sharing (with consent)

- **Values Alignment**:
  - Emphasize relationship over behavior
  - Make space for dry seasons, doubt, unanswered prayers
  - Language: "Prayer is showing up. Even in silence."

- **Community Strategy**:
  - Advisory board: Pastor, theologian, youth group leader, parent
  - Beta with 20-30 families from churches/youth groups
  - Market through church networks, not generic app stores

- **Sensitive Topics**:
  - Unanswered prayers: Make space for honest lament, not "have more faith"
  - Kids questioning faith: Create space for doubt, not condemnation
  - Family conflict: Acknowledge that prayer practices vary; respect autonomy

- **Roadmap**:
  - **MVP**: Prayer tracker with grace mechanics, family journaling, simple UI
  - **Phase 2**: Discussion forum, prayer request sharing, church integration
  - **Phase 3**: Advanced features, multiple traditions support, professional resources

### Example 2: Bible Reading App

**Your Question:**
```
We're launching a Bible reading app for ages 11-16. We want:
- Reading plans that fit their pace (not just daily)
- Progress tracking that celebrates milestones
- Community discussion (prayer, questions, encouragement)

But we're worried about:
- Making it feel grindy (scripture shouldn't be a checklist)
- Using scripture as reward/punishment
- Being preachy or judgmental

How do we integrate scripture authentically?
```

**Skill Output** (summary):

- **Theological Assessment**: 
  - ✅ Good: Age-appropriate reading plans, community discussion
  - ⚠️ Risk: Progress tracking can become legalistic if not careful
  - ✅ Strength: Multiple traditions (Catholic, Protestant, Orthodox) have rich study traditions

- **Scripture Integration**:
  - Provide full passage context, not cherry-picked verses
  - Multiple translations (ESV, NIV, NLT, The Message)
  - Commentary from diverse theological sources (not just one tradition)
  - Word study tools, cross-references, historical context
  - Never use scripture as carrot/stick (rewards/punishment)

- **Mechanic Design**:
  - Reading plans: Support various paces (daily, 2x/week, weekly, seasonal)
  - Progress tracking: Celebrate milestones, but not as moral performance
  - Language: "Scripture is living word. Come back at your pace."
  - No shame for pauses: "Stepped back? That's ok. Here's where we left off."
  - Community discussion: Honest questions, multiple interpretations welcome

- **Values Alignment**:
  - If Protestant: Emphasize personal reading, interpretation, grace
  - If Catholic: Include tradition, church teaching, sacramental connection
  - If Orthodox: Honor liturgical calendar, icon theology
  - If interfaith: Partner with leaders from each tradition; be honest about limits

- **Sensitive Topics**:
  - Difficult passages: Provide theology on violence, slavery, gender, sexuality
  - Doubt & questions: "Questioning is good. Here's how theologians grapple with this."
  - Proof-texting: "This verse is often misused. Here's the real context."
  - Trauma & scripture: "If you're healing from spiritual abuse, consider talking to a counselor."

- **Community Strategy**:
  - Advisory board: Theologian, youth pastor, parent, biblical studies teacher
  - Beta with youth groups, homeschool networks, church youth programs
  - Partner with churches for distribution (not just app stores)

- **Roadmap**:
  - **MVP**: Core reading plans, full scripture with commentary, simple discussion
  - **Phase 2**: Community features, multiple traditions, advanced study tools
  - **Phase 3**: Integration with church curriculum, small group features, church leadership dashboard

### Example 3: Values-Tracking App

**Your Question:**
```
We're building a values app for families (kids + parents, ages 7-14) 
based on Christian virtues (kindness, courage, honesty, grace, etc.). 
We want kids to reflect on living out these values—NOT earn rewards. 
We're faith-aligned, but worried about making it feel preachy or fake. 
How do we design this?
```

**Skill Output** (summary):

- **Theological Assessment**:
  - ✅ Good foundation: Virtue ethics (character development) aligns with faith
  - Red flag to avoid: Rewards for virtue (cheapens the whole thing)
  - Strength: Reflection, not performance

- **Mechanic Design**:
  - Daily reflection: "Did I see kindness today? Where? How did it feel?"
  - Journaling: Capture moments, growth, struggles
  - Family discussion: Share reflections (optional), not compete
  - No rewards: No gamification, no levels, no achievement
  - Grace mechanics: "Didn't see this virtue today? Tomorrow is new. Keep looking."

- **Values Alignment**:
  - Frame as spiritual formation (becoming like Christ), not behavior modification
  - Emphasize process, not perfection: "This is about growth, not being good"
  - Celebrate struggle: "Wrestling with courage? That's courage!"
  - Make space for grace: "We fail. God's grace covers. Try again tomorrow."

- **Content**:
  - Daily virtue reflection (not preachy; honest, humble)
  - Scripture on the virtue (context, complexity, real-life application)
  - Parent guides: How to talk about virtue at home
  - Stories: Real people living out this virtue (diverse examples)

- **Sensitive Topics**:
  - Perfectionism: "This isn't about being perfect. It's about growing."
  - Shame: "You're not a bad person if you struggle. Growth is messy."
  - Legalism: "This is invitation, not requirement. Move at your pace."
  - Diversity: "Virtue looks different in different cultures and situations."

- **Community Strategy**:
  - Advisory board: Pastor, theologian, child psychologist, parent
  - Beta with families from churches, homeschools, faith communities
  - Market through church networks and family ministry programs

- **Roadmap**:
  - **MVP**: Daily reflection, virtue journal, basic parent guides
  - **Phase 2**: Family discussion features, story library, church integration
  - **Phase 3**: Advanced curriculum, small group support, seasonal content

## What You'll Get Back

For any faith-tech product, the skill provides:

### 1. Theological Assessment
- Is this spiritually sound?
- Red flags (shame, legalism, exploitation)?
- Strengths to build on?
- Alignment with your faith tradition?

### 2. Mechanic Design
- Features that honor faith, not exploit it
- Grace-based, not performance-based
- Invitation, not obligation
- Community, not isolation
- Depth, not consumption

### 3. Values Alignment
- Does this match your faith tradition?
- Would your community recognize this as authentic?
- Are there theological trade-offs? (If yes, are they intentional?)

### 4. Community Strategy
- Advisory board structure (who to involve, why)
- Beta testing with real faith communities
- Marketing to churches, youth groups, homeschools
- Community building, not just user acquisition

### 5. Sensitive Topic Handling
- Shame & condemnation (how to avoid)
- Doubt & questions (make space)
- Spiritual abuse (recognize, prevent)
- LGBTQ+ inclusion (be explicit)
- Grief, loss, trauma (how to hold space)
- Other issues (mental health, addiction, wealth, etc.)

### 6. Implementation Roadmap
- MVP (faith-first approach)
- Phase 2 (community)
- Phase 3 (depth)
- What NOT to do (protect against exploitation)
- Metrics that matter (not DAU/MAU, but community health)

## Key Principles This Skill Uses

The skill applies these faith-tech principles:

1. **Theological Humility** — You're not the authority; get advisors
2. **Protect Against Exploitation** — Faith is vulnerable; don't exploit it
3. **Build With Community** — Don't make decisions alone
4. **Authenticity > Growth** — Slow, real growth > viral hype
5. **Honor Spiritual Realities** — Faith is real; growth is slow; grace is central

## Common Concerns & Answers

**Q: Won't this limit growth if I'm so focused on authenticity?**

A: Yes, intentionally. You'll grow slower with real community than viral hype. But your retention will be better, your community more engaged, and your values intact. Many faith-tech apps have crashed because they prioritized growth over authenticity.

**Q: Should we charge for this?**

A: Yes, but ethically. Don't charge for scripture, prayer, or basic engagement. Charge for premium features (advanced study tools, small group features, church admin tools). Be transparent about pricing.

**Q: What if our faith community disagrees on something?**

A: That's ok. Be honest about your perspective. You don't have to please everyone. A prayer app for charismatic Protestants will look different than Catholic prayer app. Own your perspective, serve your community authentically.

**Q: How do we avoid becoming irrelevant as tastes change?**

A: Stay close to your community. Talk to them. Adapt features, not theology. If your core theology is solid, you can update mechanics without abandoning your foundation.

**Q: What if we want to serve multiple faith traditions?**

A: Possible, but harder. You need advisors from each tradition. You need to ensure the app doesn't default to one tradition's assumptions. Be honest about what's universal and what's tradition-specific. Consider starting with one tradition, then expanding (not the reverse).

## Troubleshooting

### "The recommendations feel too conservative"

This skill prioritizes authenticity over growth. If you want faster growth, you may need different advisors. But be honest: is faster growth worth compromising your values?

### "We want to target churches but have no relationships"

That's a real challenge. The skill can help you plan the outreach, but you'll need to do the relational work. Consider:
- Finding a pastor/leader advisor first
- Having them introduce you to other pastors
- Starting with beta test in one church
- Growing through referrals, not ads

### "Our faith community is diverse; how do we serve everyone?"

This skill can help you think through trade-offs. You may need to focus on one tradition (serve them well) rather than trying to serve everyone generically.

### "We're worried this is exploitative. Should we even build it?"

That's the right question. Use the skill to explore that honestly. If you can't answer "why this serves my faith community" clearly, maybe reconsider.
